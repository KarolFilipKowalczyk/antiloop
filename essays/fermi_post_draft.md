# What if the universe is getting more complex over time — and that's why nobody's contacted us?

Hear me out.

We all assume the laws of physics have been the same since the Big Bang. Same rules, just more time passing. So the Fermi paradox hits hard — 13.8 billion years, hundreds of billions of galaxies, and nobody? Every solution adds some extra mechanism. Great filters, dark forests, zoo hypotheses. They all start from "the universe should have produced advanced life long ago" and then explain why we don't see it.

But what if that starting assumption is wrong?

What if the universe doesn't run on a fixed ruleset? What if it works more like a codebase that grows in complexity over time? Start with a minimal kernel — a handful of particles, a few forces, simple rules. Enough to run, but not enough to support anything we'd recognize as complex structure. Over time, the system expands. New interactions become possible. Chemistry becomes possible. Then organic chemistry. Then self-replication. Not because the ingredients finally found each other after billions of years of random shuffling — but because the *rules themselves* didn't support that level of complexity until recently.

Think of it like a game that patches itself. You can't build endgame content in the tutorial. Not because you're bad at the game, but because the mechanics literally don't exist yet.

If that's how reality works, then the Fermi paradox dissolves completely. There are no ancient galactic empires because the universe couldn't *run* galactic empires a billion years ago. Not "conditions weren't right" — the computational substrate itself was too simple. Life appears everywhere at roughly the same cosmic moment because the universe just crossed the complexity threshold required to support it.

Everyone starts the race at the same starting gun. The gun isn't a coincidence — it's the universe leveling up.

This predicts something interesting: when we eventually detect biosignatures out there, we should find lots of life at roughly similar evolutionary stages. No billion-year-old civilizations. No Kardashev Type III. Just a universe full of beings who all woke up around the same time, blinking at each other across the void, asking the same question we're asking.

The silence isn't suspicious. It's exactly what you'd expect from a universe that just learned how to be interesting.
